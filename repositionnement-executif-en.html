Executive repositioning – Engineered leadership trajectories

Beyond a certain level, a leader is not “sold”, they are positioned. Their trajectory no longer depends only on performance, but on how they are perceived, interpreted and used by the different centres of power: HQ, board, shareholders, operations, key functions.

GhostOps executive repositioning is about regaining control over this perception: narrative, proofs of authority, windows of appearance, a 6–18 month trajectory. We work where a leader’s legitimacy is built – not on cosmetic polishing of their profile.

In 30 seconds

What it is

A targeted intervention to (re)position an executive or key profile in a complex political environment: framing the narrative, staging proofs of authority, managing appearances and, where needed, scripting an exit or transfer.

Who it is for

CEOs, MDs, ExCom members, BU heads, exposed key profiles (CRO, CTO, CHRO…) whose value depends as much on internal / external perception as on raw results.

Expected outcome

A readable, ownable and protective trajectory: clarified legitimacy, reinforced support, reduced risks of disqualification or marginalisation and, as a last resort, a negotiated exit that preserves both capital and reputation.

When to activate an executive repositioning?

Typical situations

  • Highly exposed appointment (founder succession, post-crisis arrival, merger context).
  • Executive strong on business but politically weakened (perception, style, legacy).
  • ExCom member “on the hot seat” without explicit feedback or clear trajectory.
  • Key leader moved to a less visible perimeter without a coherent narrative.

Risk signals

  • Meetings where the leader is “consulted” but no longer truly listened to.
  • Important decisions taken outside their theoretical scope.
  • Multiplication of vague informal feedback: “too divisive”, “not aligned”, “draining”.
  • Progressive reduction of airtime in visible forums or in front of the board.

Four repositioning scenarios

Case #1 – A “legacy” MD in a group changing cycle

A subsidiary of 1,200 people has been led for 8 years by an MD recognised for turning the business around. The group is changing cycle: increased pressure on growth, digital transformation requirements, new ESG expectations. Internally, the MD is perceived as “solid but out of step”. Emerging talent no longer see their future with them.

Group CEO does not want to trigger social turmoil nor weaken a profitable entity, but can no longer defend status quo in front of the board.

GhostOps intervention: discreet footprint (group CEO, group HRD, a few key relays in the subsidiary). Framing of options: repositioning the MD as a “transition orchestrator” over 18 months, pairing with a deputy MD from the next generation, or preparing an honourable exit with a valorising narrative. Construction of a storyline that turns a potential eviction into an orchestrated transition. Result: MD repositioned as conductor of the handover rather than “pushed out”, subsidiary stabilised, board reassured.

Case #2 – A group CHRO turned silent scapegoat

In a listed group, the group CHRO has driven several heavy programmes (cost-cutting plans, social renegotiations, status harmonisation). Her internal image has drifted: perceived as “the executor of bad news”, she no longer manages to bring operational leaders on board for development topics.

Officially, nobody is calling for her departure, but she is no longer brought into strategic topics – only into normative and disciplinary ones.

GhostOps intervention: fine diagnosis of her real influence capital (where she still counts, where she is disqualified). Redefinition of a narrative oriented towards “building” (talent, future of work, leadership) and not only “constraint”. Scripting of targeted appearances (seminars, interventions with N-1s, CEO-sponsored dossiers). Result: progressive repositioning of the CHRO as strategic partner, without denying past decisions, but integrating them into a broader narrative arc.

Case #3 – A European Sales Director who has become “too expensive” politically

European Sales Director of a B2B division, highly performant on numbers but perceived as “individualistic” and “unmanageable”. He has direct access to several key accounts, sometimes bypasses reporting lines, and does not hesitate to challenge decisions in ExCom. His business value is high, but his political tolerance is decreasing.

The CEO hesitates: keeping him as is undermines ExCom cohesion, pushing him out weakens critical revenue and could send a dangerous message to the market.

GhostOps intervention: confidential work on three options: refocusing the director on a strategic client portfolio with a clear mandate, repositioning him as a transverse “deal maker” with guardrails, or a negotiated exit with a targeted consulting contract. Reframing the internal narrative (“indispensable but undisciplined” → “specialist activated where it makes sense”). Result: coherent trajectory, reduced risk of public clash, preservation of revenue on key accounts.

Case #4 – A high-potential female leader misaligned with the timing

An Operations Director, 42, promoted internally, is objectively one of the strongest profiles of her generation. But she emerges at a time when local HQs are being simplified and the group favours highly international profiles for top roles.

Without clarification, she risks hitting a political ceiling, then leaving for a competitor – with the conviction she has been “blocked”.

GhostOps intervention: definition with CEO and HRD of an explicit 3–5 year trajectory (potential moves, international exposure, transition roles). Construction of a “leader in accelerated build-up” narrative, with chosen windows of appearance (cross-functional projects, special missions, visibility with the board). Result: retained talent, alignment between personal ambitions and the organisation’s real horizon, reduced silent frustration.

How does a GhostOps executive repositioning work?

Step 1 – Strategic frame & red lines

  • Interviews with the sponsor (CEO, CHRO, chair) to clarify real stakes: what is negotiable and what is not.
  • Confidential interview with the executive concerned (or key profile) to understand history, assets, blind spots and plans.
  • Analysis of signals already sent (formal feedback, decisions, unspoken elements, symbolic gestures).
  • Formulation of 2–3 possible trajectories (consolidation, repositioning, orchestrated exit) with their implications.

Step 2 – Building the executive narrative

  • Definition of a clear positioning: role, unique value, perimeter, horizon.
  • Design of a narrative consistent with the company’s direction (not artificial storytelling).
  • Identification of “proofs” to highlight: results, alliances, relays, sponsors, visible projects.
  • Integration of governance, cultural and internal political constraints.

Step 3 – Orchestrating appearances & key decisions

  • Planning of exposure moments (forums, seminars, projects, formal and informal interventions).
  • Preparation of messages, Q&A, angles to emphasise or avoid depending on the audience.
  • Coordination with HR decisions (team, scope, promotion, mobility) to avoid dissonance.
  • Real-time adjustments based on reactions from the internal and external ecosystem.

Step 4 – Stabilisation or controlled exit

  • Installation of new rituals and legitimacy markers (governance, interactions, sponsorship).
  • If an exit is decided: scripting of the sequence (timeline, messages, clauses, image).
  • Transfer to the sponsor of the key reading markers to maintain narrative coherence over time.

Tangible deliverables

  • Repositioning scoping note (stakes, constraints, scenarios).
  • Leadership profile and map of internal supporters / resistances.
  • Structured executive narrative (angles, key elements, risks to contain).
  • 6–18 month exposure and decision plan (windows, milestones, messages).
  • Messaging kits for different circles (board, ExCom, teams, external stakeholders).
  • Potential exit or transfer script (options, impacts, alternative trajectories).

What GhostOps executive repositioning is not

  • A CV makeover or mere “personal branding” work on LinkedIn.
  • A disconnected coaching programme that ignores real power dynamics and governance.
  • An artificial communication campaign detached from concrete decisions.
  • A brutal eviction process disguised under “transformation” slogans.
