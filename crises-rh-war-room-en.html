HR Crises & Lockdowns – GhostOps War Room

Beyond a certain threshold, an HR crisis is no longer a matter of “social climate”: it is a direct risk to business continuity, the valuation of a unit, or the credibility of a leader. When internal power games, social tensions and defensive legal reflexes stack on top of one another, classical tools (audits, coaching, internal communication) arrive too late or too weak.

The GhostOps war room turns an unstable, fragmented and emotional situation into a steered trajectory. We organise decision-making, execution and exit, with a level of discretion compatible with high-stakes power, governance and reputation issues.

In 30 seconds

What it is

A small tactical cell (2–3 people) mandated to regain control over an HR crisis or a power lock-in: stakeholder mapping, exit scenarios, sequencing of decisions and messages, and progressive neutralisation of blocking hotspots.

Who it is for

CEOs, MDs, Group HRDs, board members facing an internal conflict, a site under high tension, a key collective in rupture, or a situation where internal inertia creates a disproportionate risk on an asset or a leader.

Expected outcome

A crisis brought back under control, decisions that can be owned, a controlled level of documentation, stabilised key players, and an organisation able to keep operating without remaining permanently “hooked” to the incident.

When to activate a GhostOps war room?

Weak signals (still reversible)

  • The same names surface in every sensitive matter, without ever being addressed head-on.
  • Key managers start taking shortcuts outside formal processes “to protect the business”.
  • Field feedback and official messages diverge, and the gap becomes structural.
  • A priority project (merger, transfer of activity, carve-out) stalls with no clear operational cause.

Strong signals (risk already engaged)

  • Open conflict involving a leader, an HRD, or a strategic collective (IT, sales, production).
  • Credible threat of external escalation: local media, social networks, unions, whistleblowers.
  • Risk of a key profile leaving at a critical timing (deal closing, post-acquisition integration).
  • Proliferation of “legalised” emails and legal references without any real alignment on an exit trajectory.

Four typical scenarios

Case #1 – Strategic industrial site on the verge of rupture

International industrial group, 420-person site in Western Europe. A transformation plan imposed by HQ (automation, reduction of temporary staff) is poorly relayed locally. The plant manager is challenged, the local HR is perceived as aligned “against the shopfloor”, and employee representative bodies (IRP) threaten to organise a highly visible action near a major customer site.

The executive committee fears a domino effect: if this site tips over, two other fragile sites may follow. The subsidiary is at year-end, margins are under strong pressure.

GhostOps intervention: 48-hour footprint (targeted interviews with leadership / HR / employee representatives / line managers). Mapping of alliances and acceptable breaking points. Co-construction with the country MD of three realistic trajectories: maintaining the plant manager (with explicit support and repositioning), ordered transition with the appointment of a deputy manager from the field, or prepared exit with a controlled narrative. Scenario design for messages to IRP, shopfloor and group, sequenced over 60 days. Result: social movement avoided, managed transition, no significant external impact.

Case #2 – Merger & local HR pinned between two systems

Growing mid-cap acquired by a listed group. The local HRD, historically very close to the founder, is caught between a corporate HQ pushing for rapid convergence of statuses and practices, and legacy managers experiencing the merger as a “takeover”.

Rumours of restructuring and layoffs spread, mid-level talents (project managers, key account managers) quietly activate their networks. The first unplanned departures occur. Internally, the board starts questioning the deal.

GhostOps intervention: tight war room with group HRD, local HRD and M&A sponsor. Clarification of HR decision lines in the post-deal context. Risk segmentation by population (key talents, managers, sensitive collectives), design of differentiated communication sequences (integration, guarantees, 18-month horizon). Implementation of short governance rituals and safeguards over 30–90 days. Result: talent leakage stopped, local HRD repositioned as a trusted relay rather than mere corporate executor.

Case #3 – Harassment allegation targeting a key manager

In a strategic sales division, an employee reports potential moral harassment by a key manager with very strong business performance. Compliance and legal take over the case, but internal positions polarise quickly: some defend the “indispensable” manager, others consider the company cannot afford a public case.

The CEO fears, on the one hand, media exposure, and on the other, a deep fracture within the sales team as a critical period approaches.

GhostOps intervention: discreet coordination with HR, legal and compliance to articulate the human, legal and business dimensions. Clarification of the sequence: rigorous handling of the report (in line with regulatory obligations), management of the manager’s presence or temporary stand-down, protection of the team and sensitive key accounts. Preparation of internal messaging (neither denial nor public trial), anticipation of different outcomes (confirmation, refutation, negotiated exit). Result: case handled in a secure framework, no internal or external runaway, business focus maintained.

Case #4 – Transformation programme blocked by a hard core

B2B services group launching a multi-country digital transformation programme. Officially, everyone is aligned. In reality, a hard core of “informal leaders” within the operations teams slows execution: minimal adoption of new tools, sabotaged meetings, ambiguous messages to the teams.

The programme sponsor reports to the board that “resistance to change” is underestimated. Project timelines explode, integrator invoices keep coming in, and the credibility of the programme erodes.

GhostOps intervention: targeted footprint on critical populations, identification of the hard core’s real motivations (fear of loss of influence, disagreement on the end-state, resentment towards HQ). Design of a de-escalation plan combining re-valuation, repositioning and, where necessary, clean exits. Narrative alignment between sponsor, HR and field management. Result: programme brought back under control, blockages neutralised without political escalation.

How does a GhostOps war room unfold?

Step 1 – Footprint (48 hours)

  • Targeted interviews with the sponsor (CEO, HRD, board member) to define scope, stakes and red lines.
  • Short interviews with 3 to 8 key actors (leadership, HR, field, sometimes employee representatives).
  • Rapid reading of existing traces: key emails, notes, minutes, critical legal documents.
  • Mapping of actors, loyalties, hotspots, windows of opportunity and fault lines.
  • Formulation of 2–3 realistic exit scenarios, with business, social and reputational impacts.

Step 2 – Operational war room (30 to 60 days)

  • Definition of a governance rhythm (short check-ins, milestones, rapid arbitrations).
  • Sequencing of sensitive decisions to avoid side effects (legal, social, reputational).
  • Preparation of key messages and scripts (leadership, managers, IRP, field, external partners).
  • Discreet alignment with legal, compliance and communications where needed.
  • Continuous adjustment based on field reactions and internal power balance.

Step 3 – Stabilisation & crisis exit

  • Implementation of “back to normal” rituals (governance, committees, managerial communication).
  • Treatment of identified relapse factors (people, processes, governance blind spots).
  • Calibration of final documentation: from minimal trace to fully opposable note, depending on exposure.
  • Transfer of key insights to the sponsor so they retain control of the narrative over time.

Tangible deliverables

  • Synthetic mapping of actors, loyalties, hotspots and associated risks.
  • Reasoned exit scenarios, with business, HR, legal and reputational impacts.
  • 30–60 day action plan, sequenced, prioritised, with key watchpoints.
  • Key messages and communication scripts for different circles (executive committee, managers, IRP, field).
  • Post-crisis governance recommendations: clarification of roles, decision circuits, safeguards.
  • Documentation level adjusted to risk: from secured orality to formal opposable note.

What the GhostOps war room is not

  • A bulky social audit produced to “cover oneself” but with no direct impact on decisions.
  • Individual coaching disconnected from power dynamics and governance constraints.
  • A cosmetic communication operation with no real ability to influence the course of events.
  • A manoeuvre outside the legal framework or contrary to obligations of social dialogue and duty of care.
